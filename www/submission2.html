Lennard-Jones Monte Carlo

Methods

In order to calculate the static properties of a theoretical ensamble of 1000 uncharged particles, a Monte-Carlo simulation based approach was used to estimate the stable states in such ensamble. It was assumed that this aprticles suffer from atractive and repulsive forces dictated by the 12-6 Lennard-Jones potential. Constant temperatures and Densities where used to explore the minimum energies and pressures generated by the ensamble after stabilization.

Discussion

System equilibration

It is seen that in our initial run (A1) where positions are assumed at random as he starting point of the simulation and temperature (T) is set to 2.0 and density (D) to 0.4, both Energy and Pressure values in the system come to equilibrium after ~20000 steps in the simulation, nonetheless it is to note that when changing the parameters (T = 4.0, D = 0.8; figure A2), the stailization takes longer, stabilizing after ~ 50000 steps into the simulation; it is to note that the values arround which the simulation stabilices also differ, in the first case being slightly arround -2350 and -2550in the second; it is to note that the pressure is in high correlation with the energy, and this is due to the fact that both the virial element and the energy depend on the sum of forces between molecules in our system (equation used to calculate pressure is shown in Equation 1).

Having such behaviour is not trivial by any means, and involves understanding exactly what is being modelled by the Lennard Jones interactions, which is the intermolecular atractions and repulsions of atoms in space, where atoms in really close proximity experience rejections and atoms in intermediate poximity experience atraction, as depicted in figure A1s; ergo, one needs to consider the two factor being modified, T and D; where D implies that more molecules are allocated in the same volume (or volume is smaller for the same number of molecules); therefore in this particular case where the reduction of the volume is not large enough for molecules to experience repulsion one can note the reduction if energy due to the increased atraction of the molecules (distances closer to the minimum point in figure A1s).

In addition it is possible to note that the number of steps required to achieve equilibrium increases by the increase in temperature, due to the fact that the possibility of accepting a non favorable step increases with the Temperature (Equation 2), therefore by random change more unfavorable steps that do not decrease the energy of the system would be accepted and from the chemistry point of view, it is more likely that our system assumes states that are not as thermodinamically stable. As an example the largest energy increase in a single step in a system (which ran for 10000 steps) that started as a continuation of our initial system (depicted in A1) was of 10.0925, which it that temperature would have the possibility of being accepted of (by using equation 2) of 1 in 10 to the power of 11 at the temperature it was run (0.4) whereas it would have been accepted once every once every 3 to the power of 6 at twice the temperature (without considering the constrains in the calculation due to the maximum precission usable by the computer).

Exploration of the State equation

When calculating the static properties of the system in response to multiple densities and pressures ( A3 ) we where able to observe as was expected that the temperature of the system is positively correlated withe pressure of it; which could empirically be understood as a higher number of micro-states outside of the most stable posible conformations.

In addition it is to note that the relationship given my the density of the system is not as simple, since on one hand, the density of neighboring particles would have an optimum, where particles are not close enough to suffer from repulsive forces but are not far enough to stop feeling attractive forces (0.7 in our system); nonetheless not all temperatures allow the system to remain in this 'perfectly' stable state and therefore high temperatures do not allow the system to manifest this minimum.

Understanding this phenomenom could be done by understanting the case where the temperature (empirically understood as vibration of atoms) is infinitely small, where no movement destabilizes the system, whereby this goup of interactions would take the system to a conformation where all particles suffer the most atraction to each other, nonetheless if any kind of vibration was added to the molecules, the atraction would decrease and the more agitation molecules experience would translate to a greater offset and therefore a higher destabilization of the system; which would explain why the system does not visit this optimal energy state a higher temperatures.

Molecular Dynamics with Gromacs

Molecular simulation of water molecules

Methodology

Unless otherwise specified the simulation for water was carried out by using the SPC/E model by setting an ensamble of 216 molecules at 300k and 1 Atm of pressure, where conditions where stabilized by a series of runs initially involving energy minimization with the method of steepest descend followed by temperature stabilization by running an NVT simulation with Berendsen as a thermostat for 100ps and an NPT simulation for the same period of time in the simulation; Afterwards equilibrated systems where run as an NVT simulation using nose-hoover thermostat to derive the dynamic properties of the system for 400ps, only the last 200ps where used to make the calculations; all configurations files and scripts are available upon request.

Temperature Dependent Dynamics

To determine the temperature dependent dynamic properties four runs where done with equilibration and run temperatures set to four different temperatures (260k, 280k, 300k and 380k).

From the ensambles we where able to note several properties that changed with the changes in temperature of the system, at first one can note that there are structural chenges at several levels in our model system, where one can observe the changes in state in our model both as as a reduction in the diffussion coefficient as temperatures decrease (table 1) and as a shift in the pair coorelation curve that resembles more the one of an ideal gas.

Diffusion experiments

To calculate the diffusion of water, the same model as reviously mentioned was used with conditions of 25 C and 1 atm in an NPT ensamble, using the Parrinello Rahman barostat.

Making use of this ensamble a diffussion coefficient of 2.4661 (+/- 0.1884 1e-5 cm2/s) was measured, which closely resembles previously reported values for this simple water molecule model; in contrast with 2.49, as published by Mahoney and Jorgensen (Mahoney and Jorgensen, 2001). In additional it is psible to compare this value with the one derived from more complex models of water molecules where virtual atoms are added to increase the resemblance to experimental properties at the expense of an increase computational complexity; as such the TIP5P model pruduces a measured value of 2.62 at the same conditions of pressure and temperature, which depending on the aplication might or not be neglectable (further emphasizing the need to choose the appropiate model depending on the application).

Why not use Metropolis Monte Carlo ?

By definition the monte carlo methods do not sample our ensamble in time and as such can not provide information regarding the dinamic properties of the system, in other words, since it does not have the conception of time in the simulation; cannot provide information regarding processes that occur through time, such as diffussion.

Why should maximum cut offs of the interactions be smaller than half the box size ?

The answer lies in the fact that when considering a periodic boundary condition in our simulation, a cutoff higher than that would mean that an atom could exert forces with itself in the neighboring box, which in no way would resemble a system in the real world.

Molecular Dynamics of Carbon Dioxide

The modelling of liquid CO2 gets to be challenging in several regards, first of all, it is necessary to restrict the posible conformations of the molecule for it to ressemble the native conformation as a plannar molecule, and in addition the fact that CO2 only acquires its liquid state at high pressure makes the equilibration steps challenging to accomplish (Under more than 5 atm of pressure and temperatures close to -60 C).

Approach to model the molecule

In order to solve the priorly mentioned problems several complementary approaches where used; at first the planarity of the molecule was modelled as a pair of entities with constricted distance instead of using the empiric aproach of binding carbons and oxygen to form the molecule; therefore the mass component of the molecule is given by this two massive elements and the charge (and inter-molecular interaction component) of the carbon and oxygen atoms are given by virtual sites aling this couple of massive atoms.

Several modifications where made from the molecular dinamics parameters files published originally for argon molecules in order to make the model viable (not crash at least); Importantly it was required to change the reference pressure of the system; since liquid argon is obtainable at lower pressures as the ones required for CO2, on the other hand since higher temperatures are also required, the overall system changes much more rapidly than the case of Argon and as such a much finer equilibration ois necessary, since large step sizes and coupling coefficents (tau_p and tau_t) lead to the crash of the system (makes it 'explote') when submitting the system to our particular conditions of 20 bar and 250k (even during the equilibration steps) which makes it particularly hard to test most of the proposed situations.

One can get to observe that with the many modifications made one is able to have a stable system with the sequential use of energy minimization, NVT and NPT simulations (stages depicted in figures C1 and C2); nonetheless this stability is accompained by large oscilations in the measured properties despite the averaging over time in constant values.

It was proven to be of ulterior importance the need to explicitly exclude interactions within our molecule, due to the fact that the lack of bonds that was usefull to state the planarity of the molecule does not exclude them from interacting electrostatically with each other.

Annealing Simulation

In order to attempt to visualize a change of state in our model an annealing was simulated where a gradient of temperature was applied to the system from 250k to 200k in 500 ps and the properties of the system where measured, It is posible to note the decrease in movement of the particles, as expressed by pressure and energy of the system and furthermore (fig C4), visualization of the actual movement of the molecules is also provided in figure C3.

Remarks

As a concluding remark it is to note that most likely this model is not accurate and most certainly does not resemble the behaviour of liquid CO2, nonetheless it proved to be an really interesting tool to verify the importance of sequential and carefully chosen equilibration steps to generate a measurable system.
