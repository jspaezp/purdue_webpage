Structure of the Apoptosome Complex

Apoptosome

Apoptosis is a highly regulated process that involves the organized killing of a cell by a series of proteins in a cell called caspases. One of the main regulators of the process is the assembly of the structure known as the apoptosome, which is a large heptameric structure composed of Apaf-1 (apoptosis protease activating factor 1) and cytochrome c (Fig. 5), whose function lies in the later activation of the pro-caspase 9 to start programed cell death (Yuan, 2013a).

In order to fulfill its function the complex must assamble form its constitutive components, by which Cytochrome C, which is originally present in the mitochondria, binds to the loop regions of two beta-propeller structures in APAF1 (Fig 6). For the stabilization of this complex a heme group there are a series of reactions that need to take place, where APAF1 needs ATP to oligomerize and start the complex formation (Yuan, 2013b), this ATP molecule binds deeply into the molecule with its purine side while exposing the phosphates to the exterior, which then is occluded by other members of the protein, now shown (Fig 7).

Once the initial interaction between Cytochrome C and APAF1 has taken place the pressence of a heme molecule in the Cytochrome is (suspected to be) needed to carry out an aminoacid exchange and acquire a functional conformation.
