Potassium Channel

Biological membranes are made of phospholipids, molecules that present a large hydrophobic region and a small hydrophyllic region, this physicochemical nature, in addition to an aqueous environment favour the formation of bilayers, where the hydrophobic regions are bundled together and the hydroplillic region face the environment; Due to the large hydrophobic region highly hidrophillic compounds do not readily cross the membrane, nonetheless this permeability is necessary for some cellular processes.

The role of transmembrane channels is to provide stable intermediate states for molecules that would not normally cross the cell membrane lipid bilayer, where several factors that provide thermodinamical stability to this kind of molecules can be found and are evident when looking at the crystal structure of such kinds of proteins (Choe, 2002).

The potassium channel of Streptomyces lividans is a transmembrane protein that fulfills its role as a potassium specific channel, as such there are several characteristics inherent to it that are found to be extremely interesting that allow this protein to fulfill its biological role (Doyle, et.al 1998).

First of all, for the channel to perform its function, it must be inserted in the cell membrane, and as such it must stabilize its pressence in the membrane by associating its exterior, on the sides with the hydrophobic tails of the membrane and the lipids, which can be seen in Figure 1 where it is evident that aminoacids sidechains exposed to the lipid bilayer are of hydripobic nature and the ones exposed to the aqueous environment are of hydriphillic nature, interestingly sidechains in the inteface region are of amphipatic nature, where one region of the side chain is hydropboic but also posseses a region where hydrogen bonding could be carried out with the environment.

In addition it is possible to appreciate that the channel is formed by four monomeric units, that ensemble due to shape complementarity in addition to hydrophobic interactions among the subunits Fig.2.

Regarding the channel properties of the protein it is necesarry to note the interactions that stabilize charged ions in a hydrohobic space, where energy must be compensated for the desolvation of ions, this is carried out in two different regions of the channel, Fig 3 shows how the ions are selected on the basis of their charge and size by interacting in a narrow groove that stabilizes ions by interacting with the oxygen atoms in the backbone protein's backbone. In addition, past the selectivity filter, it is possible to show how the net dipolar moment of alpha helices also stabilizes the ions in an aqueous cavity in the multimer (due to the ordered peptide bonds in the structure's backbone).
