Lennard-Jones Monte Carlo

Methods

In order to calculate the static properties of a theoretical ensamble of 1000 uncharged particles, a Monte-Carlo simulation based approach was used to estimate the stable states in such ensamble. It was assumed that this aprticles suffer from atractive and repulsive forces dictated by the 12-6 Lennard-Jones potential. Constant temperatures and Densities where used to explore the minimum energies and pressures generated by the ensamble after stabilization.

Discussion

System equilibration

It is seen that in our initial run (A1) where positions are assumed at random as he starting point of the simulation and temperature (T) is set to 2.0 and density (D) to 0.4, both Energy and Pressure values in the system come to equilibrium after ~20000 steps in the simulation, nonetheless it is to note that when changing the parameters (T = 4.0, D = 0.8; figure A2), the stailization takes longer, stabilizing after ~ 50000 steps into the simulation; it is to note that the values arround which the simulation stabilices also differ, in the first case being slightly arround -2350 and -2550in the second; it is to note that the pressure is in high correlation with the energy, and this is due to the fact that both the virial element and the energy depend on the sum of forces between molecules in our system (equation used to calculate pressure is shown in Equation 1).

Having such behaviour is not trivial by any means, and involves understanding exactly what is being modelled by the Lennard Jones interactions, which is the intermolecular atractions and repulsions of atoms in space, where atoms in really close proximity experience rejections and atoms in intermediate poximity experience atraction, as depicted in figure A1s; ergo, one needs to consider the two factor being modified, T and D; where D implies that more molecules are allocated in the same volume (or volume is smaller for the same number of molecules); therefore in this particular case where the reduction of the volume is not large enough for molecules to experience repulsion one can note the reduction if energy due to the increased atraction of the molecules (distances closer to the minimum point in figure A1s).

In addition it is possible to note that the number of steps required to achieve equilibrium increases by the increase in temperature, due to the fact that the possibility of accepting a non favorable step increases with the Temperature (Equation 2), therefore by random change more unfavorable steps that do not decrease the energy of the system would be accepted and from the chemistry point of view, it is more likely that our system assumes states that are not as thermodinamically stable. As an example the largest energy increase in a single step in a system (which ran for 10000 steps) that started as a continuation of our initial system (depicted in A1) was of 10.0925, which it that temperature would have the possibility of being accepted of (by using equation 2) of 1 in 10 to the power of 11 at the temperature it was run (0.4) whereas it would have been accepted once every once every 3 to the power of 6 at twice the temperature (without considering the constrains in the calculation due to the maximum precission usable by the computer).

Exploration of the State equation

When calculating the static properties of the system in response to multiple densities and pressures ( A3 ) we where able to observe as was expected that the temperature of the system is positively correlated withe pressure of it; which could empirically be understood as a higher number of micro-states outside of the most stable posible conformations.

In addition it is to note that the relationship given my the density of the system is not as simple, since on one hand, the density of neighboring particles would have an optimum, where particles are not close enough to suffer from repulsive forces but are not far enough to stop feeling attractive forces (0.7 in our system); nonetheless not all temperatures allow the system to remain in this 'perfectly' stable state and therefore high temperatures do not allow the system to manifest this minimum.

Understanding this phenomenom could be done by understanting the case where the temperature (empirically understood as vibration of atoms) is infinitely small, where no movement destabilizes the system, whereby this goup of interactions would take the system to a conformation where all particles suffer the most atraction to each other, nonetheless if any kind of vibration was added to the molecules, the atraction would decrease and the more agitation molecules experience would translate to a greater offset and therefore a higher destabilization of the system; which would explain why the system does not visit this optimal energy state a higher temperatures.

Molecular simulation of water molecules

Methodology

Unless otherwise specified the simulation fo water was carried out by using the SPC/E model by setting an ensamble of 216 molecules at 300k and 1 Atm of pressure, where conditions where stabilized by a series of runs initially involving energy minimization with the method of steepest descend followed by temperature stabilization by running an NVT simulation with Berendsen as a thermostat for 100ps and an NPT simulation for the same period of time in the simulation; Afterwards equilibrated systems where run as an NVT simulation using nose-hoover thermostat to derive the dynamic properties of the system for 400ps, only the last 200ps where used to make the calculations; all configurations files and scripts are available upon request.

Temperature Dependent Dynamics

To determine the temperature dependent dynamic properties four runs where done with equilibration and run temperatures set to four different temperatures (260k, 280k, 300k and 380k).
